## What changed from your v1 draft (read this first)

Eleven substantive moves, in rough order of importance:

1. **The name and the prognosis are softer than stated.** "Muerte fulminante del laurel" is a **vernacular label coined by Salazar himself** — a private arborist/consultant (firm *Consultoría y Manejo Integral de Áreas Verdes*), with phytopathologist **Vicente Díaz Balderas** — **not a term in the scientific literature**, where the disease is "muerte descendente" (dieback). Salazar's own prognosis is a **range of "weeks to years,"** not a fixed 2-year clock, and he claims a **~60% save rate** with treatment ("no recipe, no guarantee"). A **2025 peer-reviewed survey of 387 *F. microcarpa* in Cuernavaca found only ~5% dieback citywide**, with poor siting and bad pruning — not the pathogen — as the dominant damage. The "2 years, destined to die" framing is a practitioner estimate, not validated science, and is **tree- and management-dependent**.

2. **A new, cheap, decisive diagnostic gate: ambrosia beetles.** Morelos issued a **June 2026 phytosanitary alert** for ambrosia beetles (*Euwallacea* sp. and *Xyleborus affinis*) killing mature trees "in weeks to months" — which fits "fulminante" better than canker alone. The confirmed fungi (*Lasiodiplodia*, *Neoscytalidium*) are **not** the beetles' farmed symbionts, so beetles are most likely **opportunistic secondary accelerators of a canker-driven decline** (the documented *Ficus carica* + *Lasiodiplodia* + bark-beetle pattern in Italy). This is **cheap to rule in or out** with a 30-minute trunk hole-count, and it **changes the tactics** — see §2.

3. **The "no cure on any host, ever" claim was slightly too absolute.** There is **one** peer-reviewed therapeutic result on *established* Botryosphaeriaceae canker: injected **phosphorous acid shrank already-established madrone cankers (*Neofusicoccum arbuti*) ~50–70%** for ≥1 year (Elliott & Edmonds 2008). But it is **partial suppression via induced host callusing/defense, not fungicidal eradication**, on a far smaller host. Reframe the claim: *no fungicide **eradicates** established infection; one trial showed partial **suppression** via host defense.*

4. **The laurel-wilt / shot-hole-borer analog in v1 §2 was over-weighted as a *disease* analog.** *Laurel de la India* = *Ficus microcarpa* = **Moraceae**, not Lauraceae — true **laurel wilt** (*Harringtonia/Raffaelea lauricola* + *Xyleborus glabratus*) is an avocado/Lauraceae disease and a **botanical false-friend**. Keep the beetle angle, but as a *vector to diagnose*, not as a disease parallel.

5. **Phosphite was oversold.** Its strong, multi-year injection evidence is **all against *Phytophthora* (an oomycete)**. No solid trial shows phosphite controls *Lasiodiplodia*/*Neoscytalidium*. Keep it only as a plausible low-risk **defense primer/vigor adjunct** — don't imply it treats the canker.

6. **The PTA-271 "63–75% canker reduction" figure could not be independently confirmed;** the verifiable evidence is mechanistic/qualitative and cultivar-dependent. Flag the number as unverified.

7. **Stronger, more on-target biocontrols now lead the list:** ***Bacillus velezensis* YK268** (~72% **field** control of *Lasiodiplodia* branch blight on a woody host — avocado) and ***B. halotolerans* BJ-3** (2025, ~72% field control of grapevine Botryosphaeria dieback). And ***Neoscytalidium*-specific** agents (*T. asperellum* K1-02, *Paenibacillus polymyxa* AF01) that v1 didn't cover — important, since *Neoscytalidium* is a confirmed pathogen here.

8. **Mexican field data caveat on *Trichoderma*:** Veracruz trials found it controls *Lasiodiplodia* **in vitro but not in the field** for this branch pathogen. So *Trichoderma* is a **fresh-cut wound protectant (70–100%, ~21 days), not a stand-alone therapy** — exactly where v1 should pin it.

9. **Sanitation pruning is a distinct, co-equal lever**, not a sub-bullet of crown reduction: cut ≥15–25 cm below visible canker into clean wood, sterilize tools every cut, destroy debris. And **cap crown reduction well below the ANSI A300 25%/season limit** for these stressed trees, framed as multi-year *retrenchment*; mature-tree crown-reduction recovery evidence is thin/observational.

10. **Don't over-rank mycorrhizal inoculation** — on *established* trees it raises colonization but shows **no measured growth benefit**. Keep it a cheap adjunct, not a pillar.

11. **Genetics section upgraded to a concrete 3-tier protocol** with numbers: **air-layering ~80–90% vs. stem cuttings ~18–20%** on *F. microcarpa* (air-layer is decisively the method) → **tissue culture/micropropagation** backup → **cryopreservation** insurance, plus a **hot-water clean-up step (~50 °C/30–45 min)** to bank *pathogen-free* clones. Also: ***Neoscytalidium dimidiatum* is a human opportunistic pathogen** — pruning crews need gloves/masks.

Frontier methods (RNAi/SIGS directly on *Lasiodiplodia*; mycovirus hypovirulence in *Botryosphaeria dothidea*; nano-fungicides hitting *Neoscytalidium*) are **real science but lab/greenhouse-stage** — not deployable on a 20 m tree. Documented for completeness in §8.

# Muerte descendente del laurel (Ficus dieback) — Rescue Dossier

## TL;DR

1. **Salazar is essentially right that there is no cure** — but "no cure" means *no fungicide eradicates the fungus from colonized wood*, not "nothing can be done." This is a **stress-opportunist** disease: the fungus lives latently inside healthy *Ficus* for years and only turns lethal under heat/drought/root stress. A tree made vigorous enough walls off and calluses over cankers. The whole strategy is **breaking the stress assumption** behind the 2-year clock.
2. **First, diagnose — don't assume.** Two cheap checks reshape everything: (a) confirm it's the canker complex and not predominantly **ambrosia beetles** (Morelos has an active 2026 alert) by counting trunk entry holes; (b) get a **written per-tree risk assessment** because a declining 20 m tree near people is a *living hazard*.
3. **Highest-value, best-proven, lowest-risk intervention = root-zone rehabilitation** (decompaction, radial trenching + compost/biochar, woodchip mulch, corrected irrigation). Strongest *mature-tree* recovery evidence of anything researched, and it directly removes the predisposing stress — which is *also* the ethanol signal that recruits beetles.
4. **Preserve the genetics now** via air-layering (~80–90% success) — a near-certain win that de-risks every other decision. Bank pathogen-free clones with a hot-water clean-up step.
5. **Sanitation surgery + wound protection (*Trichoderma*/*Bacillus*) buys real time;** injections and dramatic pollarding are the **weakest** bets.
6. **Honest outcome:** you likely won't keep all five exactly as they are; the most decayed may have to come down for safety. But **slowing or arresting decline on the better trees is realistic, and preserving every one genetically is near-certain.**

---

## 1. What the disease actually is

The popular name *"muerte fulminante del laurel"* (also *"muerte descendente"*) covers a **Botryosphaeriaceae canker/dieback complex.** The "fulminante" branding is **local and vernacular** — coined by Cuernavaca arborist **Biól. Pedro Salazar Hernández** (who first saw it on a Jiutepec laurel in 2011) together with phytopathologist **Vicente Díaz Balderas**. The peer-reviewed Mexican term is **"muerte descendente"** (dieback). Confirmed pathogens in Mexican peer-reviewed work:

| Pathogen | Status | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| ***Lasiodiplodia theobromae*** | **Proven** on *F. benjamina*, Morelos | UAEM thesis (morphology + ITS); first MX report on Ficus; 750 trees surveyed in eastern Morelos |
| ***Neoscytalidium dimidiatum*** | **Proven** on *F. benjamina*, Sonora | Rev. Chapingo 2017: ITS/LSU/RPB2 + **Koch's postulates**; "sooty canker" (black arthroconidia under bark = the layperson's "hongo negro") |
| *Botryosphaeria dothidea, Neofusicoccum mediterraneum, N. parvum* | Proven on *F. microcarpa* — **Italy/California, not MX** | MDPI *Plants* 2022; pathogenicity confirmed |
| *Phytophthora, Ganoderma, Verticillium, Fusarium* | **Unconfirmed early complex** | 2007 Tehuacán isolations; no Koch's postulates; likely mixed/secondary |
| Aphids / scale / thrips (+ secondary **sooty mold / fumagina**) | **Locally implicated, mostly secondary** | UAEM: 99% of surveyed trees had thrips; "hongo negro" in the press conflates the *Neoscytalidium* sooty canker with cosmetic sooty mold on insect honeydew |

**The decisive biological fact** (independently stated across dozens of sources): Botryosphaeriaceae are **opportunistic — they attack trees already stressed by drought, heat, root damage, or compaction.** They persist as suppressed endophytes for years; severe stress flips them to the host-killing phase (confirmed experimentally: combined heat + water stress worsens Botryosphaeria dieback). *"If a tree is healthy enough, it may callus over cankers completely."* **This is the entire basis for the rescue strategy.**

**On the prognosis:** No peer-reviewed source states a fixed "2-year death." Salazar himself describes a range from **"tres o cuatro semanas"** (fulminant cases) to **"lentamente durante años."** The **2025 Rev. Chapingo survey of 387 *F. microcarpa* in Cuernavaca** found mean live-crown ratio 70%, crown density 65%, and **only ~5% dieback citywide** — the dominant problems were reduced growing space (55%) and bad topiary pruning (18%), not the pathogen. The single biggest documented risk multiplier is **heavy pruning** (Veracruz citrus work: intense pruning raised *Lasiodiplodia* mortality up to ~35%/yr by opening infection courts). So prognosis hinges on how the trees are managed — which is good news for an intervention.

**Local policy context:** Cuernavaca and neighboring municipalities have **banned new *F. benjamina* plantings** and lean toward **removal of advanced cases + replacement with native amates**. But in **April 2026** the municipality publicly admitted a removal dictamen "could have been strengthened" and was "not conclusive about the immediacy of removal, since the trees still had estimated remaining life" — i.e., **there is active local criticism that removal is sometimes applied prematurely to treatable trees.** Bank the genetics (§7) so that keep-vs-remove is never an all-or-nothing call.

## 2. Diagnose before you treat — two cheap gates that reshape the plan

### Gate A — Is it canker, or canker + ambrosia beetles?

Morelos issued a **June 2026 phytosanitary alert** (CESVMOR/SENASICA-adjacent) naming **ambrosia beetles — *Euwallacea* sp. and *Xyleborus affinis*** — drying mature trees "in weeks or months." *Ficus microcarpa* is a confirmed reproductive host of the *Euwallacea fornicatus* complex; PSHB has been in Mexico (Baja California) since 2015. Ambrosia beetles home in on **ethanol from stressed/cankered trees**, then add vascular fungal damage — producing sudden whole-limb collapse. The confirmed fungi here are **not** the beetles' symbionts (*Fusarium euwallaceae*, *Harringtonia lauricola*), so the most likely picture is **a canker-driven decline with beetles as opportunistic secondary accelerants** — exactly the documented *Ficus carica* + *Lasiodiplodia* + bark-beetle pattern in Italy.

**Why it matters:** if beetles are an active driver, the one intervention with *therapeutic* evidence on already-infested trees is **emamectin benzoate trunk injection (± propiconazole)** — and sanitation (chipping infested wood) becomes mandatory. If there are no holes, beetles aren't the lever and you don't waste money on them.

**30-minute field protocol (do this on all five trees):**
1. **Entry/exit holes** — perfectly round pinholes ~0.85–2 mm on trunk/scaffold limbs.
2. **Signatures** — white crystalline "sugar volcanoes," dark wet staining, gumming, fine frass / "sawdust toothpicks" at hole mouths.
3. **Under-bark** — scrape around a hole: dark brown-black gallery staining; cut a cross-section for branching galleries.
4. **Quantify** — count holes on the lower 2 m. Rules of thumb from the literature: **>10 attacks/lower-2 m** = chemical-treatment threshold; **>150 holes + branch dieback** = vascular system likely too compromised to save.
5. **ID** — trap beetles / culture gallery wood; loop in **CESVMOR / SENASICA SIRVEF** (they run official *Euwallacea* surveillance in Morelos, will ID for free, and may have reporting obligations).

**Interpretation:** many fresh holes + sugar/frass + dark galleries = active beetle-fungus complex → add EB injection (if light/moderate) + strict sanitation. Cankers/cracking/dieback **without** holes/frass = pure Botryosphaeria canker → beetles are not the lever; do **not** conflate with the *Lauraceae* "laurel rojo" program (that's *Xyleborus glabratus* + *Raffaelea* on avocado/true laurels — a different complex).

### Gate B — Is each tree a structural hazard?
Commission a **written ISA Tree Risk Assessment (TRAQ) per tree**, triaged by what's underneath each one (see §9). This can override everything else.

## 3. The curative question — answered six ways, same core answer

**No fungicide *eradicates* an established infection from wood and cures the tree. Validated chemical success is almost entirely PREVENTIVE — protecting fresh pruning wounds from new spore infection.** Confirmed across the literature:

- **Extension consensus is unanimous** (UC IPM, UMass, Virginia Tech): *"there is no fungicide that can cure Botryosphaeria canker"*; chemistry is preventive, the only thing that removes established infection is mechanical (surgery into clean wood).
- **Mechanism, now directly confirmed:** the 2023 propiconazole laurel-wilt reevaluation found injected concentrations in **mature** trees fall **below the fungicidal threshold** due to "restricted mobility within the tree vascular system." Exactly v1's argument.
- **In-vitro ≠ in-planta:** Mexican screens show cyprodinil+fludioxonil, mancozeb, tebuconazole at ~100% mycelial inhibition in a Petri dish; dragon-fruit field trials on *Neoscytalidium* stems never exceeded ~65%. The collapse from plate to standing wood is the rule.
- **Mexican *Trichoderma* field caveat:** Veracruz trials found *Trichoderma* controls *Lasiodiplodia* in vitro but **not in the field** for this branch pathogen — because the infection sits in wood the biocontrol can't reach.

**The one genuine nuance (new in v2):** in **Pacific madrone** (*Neofusicoccum arbuti*, a true Botryosphaeriaceae), **injected phosphorous acid reduced the size of *artificially established* cankers ~50–70%** for ≥1 year (Elliott & Edmonds 2008). This is real **therapeutic suppression of an established Botryosphaeriaceae canker** — but (a) partial, not a cure; (b) the authors attribute it to **induced host defense/callusing, not fungicidal eradication**; (c) madrone cankers are far shallower than a 20 m *Ficus* trunk. It tempers "never any therapeutic effect on any host," but does **not** rescue "cure."

**Bottom line for these five trees:** trunk injection is the wrong tool for *curing* them. At most it might induce some host defense/callusing and *partially slow* canker expansion — and injection wounds are themselves cumulative damage (drilling embolizes xylem, holes can't be reused, drilling can breach decay-walling barriers), worst in already-declining low-vigor trees. The exception is the **beetle case** (§2): if confirmed and light/moderate, **emamectin benzoate** injection has therapeutic data — but it works by killing the beetle, not curing the wilt.

## 4. What the evidence DOES support — ranked by proof strength × low risk

### (A) Root-zone rehabilitation — **primary effort; best mature-tree evidence**
Because this is stress-driven decline, fixing the root environment has the strongest peer-reviewed recovery data of anything researched — and it removes both the stress that lets the fungus win **and** the ethanol signal that recruits beetles.
- **Woodchip mulch** over the root zone: increased total root surface area **up to ~195%** vs turf competition; most cost-effective single treatment (multiple AUF studies).
- **Radial-trench soil replacement / air-spade decompaction** on mature trees: denser, deeper roots and larger growth rings (Watson — reported fine-root density up sharply and rooting depth up substantially; **verify the +320%/+68% figures against the PDF**). Air-spade + biochar + woodchip mulch was the optimal combination over a 5-year ISA study.
- **Biochar**: disease-severity meta-analysis ~47% reduction (≈59% at 3–5% soil application) with ~44% biomass gain; restored vigor in *decline-disease* bayberry.

**Protocol:** air-spade the root plates → expose the flare / find girdling or buried-flare roots (a classic, fixable *Ficus* killer) → radial trenches (~start 3 m from trunk, ~60 cm deep) backfilled with compost/biochar → vertical mulching → coarse organic mulch kept **off the trunk** (≥10 in clear) → **deep but never waterlogged** irrigation (moisten 30–45 cm deep, water the dripline not the trunk, let the top ~15 cm dry between cycles). Optional mycorrhizae/*Bacillus* — see caveat in §4D.

### (B) Sanitation surgery + staged canopy reduction — **buys real time**
- **Remedial surgery is the only "curative" act:** prune cankered limbs **~15–25 cm below the visible canker into clean, undiscolored wood**; **sterilize tools between every cut** (10% bleach/alcohol — the pathogen contaminates the next 2–3 cuts); **destroy/chip debris** (spore + beetle source). Feasible only where cankers are on removable limbs, not the main trunk.
- **Staged crown reduction** lowers transpirational demand on damaged xylem and reduces sail/risk. **Cap well below the ANSI A300 25%/season limit** for these stressed trees; frame as multi-year **retrenchment** (grow a lower replacement crown over seasons), not a one-time mass removal. *Caveat:* sound physiology, but mature-tree crown-reduction recovery evidence is **thin and observational** — treat as buying time, and avoid over-reduction (it produces weak, failure-prone epicormic shoots).
- **Two hard rules:** prune **only in dry weather** (humidity spreads spores onto cuts) and **never in the hot/peak-spore season.** Crews wear **gloves/masks** — *Neoscytalidium dimidiatum* is a human opportunistic pathogen.

### (C) Wound protectants & resistance primers — **adjuncts, not cures**
Apply to every fresh cut to block re-infection:
- ***Trichoderma*** (T. asperelloides/atroviride/canadense): **70–100% pruning-wound protection** for ~21 days in trials vs this fungus family; ~88–100% in some field studies — **the most relevant biocontrol here, but as wound protection only** (it does not clear colonized wood, per the Mexican field data).
- ***Bacillus velezensis*** YK268: ~**72% field control** of *Lasiodiplodia* branch blight on a woody host (avocado) — the most on-target *Bacillus*; ***B. halotolerans*** BJ-3 (2025): ~72% field control of grapevine Botryosphaeria dieback. **For *Neoscytalidium* specifically:** *T. asperellum* K1-02 (72–84% in vitro), *Paenibacillus polymyxa* AF01.
- **Fungicide paste** on cuts (thiophanate-methyl, tebuconazole, pyraclostrobin+boscalid) and **Cu/chitosan wound paint** as topical protectants.
- ***Bacillus subtilis*** PTA-271 (immune priming + phytotoxin detoxification; the "63–75%" figure is **unverified** — its published evidence is mechanistic/cultivar-dependent) and **phosphite** as low-risk defense primers. **Note:** phosphite's strong evidence is against *Phytophthora*, **not** Botryosphaeriaceae — keep it as a vigor/ISR adjunct, don't promise canker control.

### (D) Vigor / soil-biology adjuncts — **plausible, modest**
- **Arbuscular mycorrhizae**: strong evidence for drought/stress resilience generally, but **established-tree field trials show colonization up with no measured growth benefit** — a cheap adjunct, *not* a pillar.
- **Biostimulants / compost teas**: support the "raise vigor so the tree walls it off" thesis; no direct anti-canker proof.

### (E) Preserve the genetics NOW — **the guaranteed win** (see §7 for the full protocol)

## 5. If beetles are confirmed (Gate A positive)

Layer onto — don't replace — the canker plan:
- **Emamectin benzoate (± propiconazole) trunk injection** on **light-to-moderately** infested trees (well under the ~150-hole ceiling): the rare intervention with **therapeutic** data on already-infested standing trees (sycamore: reduced beetle colonization and *Fusarium* success; retreat ~every 2 yr). Above the ceiling, no treatment is expected to save the individual.
- **Mandatory sanitation:** any infested/cankered wood **chipped <5 cm and solarized/bagged (~55 °C, 4–6 wk)**, never stacked or moved — chipping cut beetle emergence **>97%**; burning un-chipped logs is ineffective (beetles escape first).
- **Protect survivors:** preventive **pyrethroid bark sprays (bifenthrin)** timed to beetle flight on the least-affected trees and valuable neighbors.
- **Reframe root/vigor rehab as anti-beetle too** — lowering drought stress lowers the ethanol plume that recruits attacks.

## 6. The drastic "save" — hard pollarding (last resort, trial only)

*Ficus* is one of Earth's best resprouters and tolerates heavy heading as a street tree. **But** resprout reliability falls sharply with size/age, a stressed low-reserve 20 m tree is the worst-case candidate, and a heavy pollard opens dozens of fresh infection courts for the very fungus you're fighting. **If attempted:** trial on the single most-declined tree first; stage it (retrenchment) over 2–3 seasons; time to spring/dry weather; biocontrol every wound; watch for epicormic response before risking the others. Lower-risk than pollarding for the same goal: **retrenchment crown reduction** (§4B).

## 7. Preserve the genetics — the 3-tier protocol (do all in parallel this season)

**Tier 1 — Air-layering (PRIMARY, now).** *F. microcarpa* air-layers at **~80–90%** vs only **~18–20%** for stem cuttings — air-layering is decisively the right method (it's the classic bonsai technique on this species). Select healthy 1-yr-old branches **from the least-affected wood of each of the five trees** (capture all five genotypes, labeled); girdle a ~2.5 cm bark ring to cambium; apply IBA rooting hormone; pack moist sphagnum; wrap; keep moist; sever below roots once rooted (weeks). Best in spring/early-warm season. **Take dozens per tree from canker-free wood only.**

**Tier 2 — Tissue culture / micropropagation (BACKUP + clean-up).** *Ficus* micropropagates well (MS + ~2 mg/L BAP for shoots; ~0.4 mg/L IBA for rooting). **Meristem-tip culture additionally cleans systemic pathogens.** A Morelos/UNAM/UAEM tissue-culture lab can execute this for near-unlimited true-to-type, disease-free clones.

**Tier 3 — Cryopreservation (long-term insurance, institutional).** Shoot-tip cryo by encapsulation-dehydration / droplet-vitrification for indefinite banking; needs a cryo-capable lab to adapt a protocol (no published *F. microcarpa*-specific one found).

**Clean-up step (combine with Tiers 1–2):** **hot-water-treat dormant propagules (~50 °C / 30–45 min)** — shown to eradicate Botryosphaeriaceae from cuttings — so the clones you bank are pathogen-free.

**Ficus-specific structural bonus:** aerial **prop-root induction** (PVC pipe + sphagnum, ± rain funnel) re-supports weakening limbs — in *F. rubiginosa*, **26/30 (87%) aerial roots reached the ground in one year vs 0/10 controls.** Cheap, low-tech, and a genuine safety mitigation for heavy limbs (extrapolate to *microcarpa* with care).

## 8. Frontier methods — real science, NOT deployable on a 20 m tree (logged for completeness)

- **RNAi / spray-induced gene silencing:** there is **direct work on your pathogen** — a Tef-1α RNA hairpin against *L. theobromae* + *N. parvum*, and an **LtmilR2 RNA duplex + nanocarrier proposed as an "RNA-based fungicide" for grapevine canker.** Lab/proof-of-concept; penetrating colonized 20 m xylem with sprayed dsRNA isn't feasible today. 3–7 yr horizon.
- **Mycovirus hypovirulence** (the chestnut-blight model): a hypovirulence-conferring mycovirus **exists in *Botryosphaeria dothidea***, and a mycovirus was found in *Lasiodiplodia* (LpMyV1, 2025) — but none is characterized/formulated for *L. theobromae*/*N. dimidiatum*, and vegetative incompatibility blocks field spread. Speculative.
- **Nano-fungicides:** Cu nanoparticles inhibit *L. theobromae* (~52% at 4000 ppm); **ZnO/CaO–ZnO suspensions inhibit *Neoscytalidium dimidiatum*** — usable only as topical wound paint, not a systemic cure.
- **Resistant genotypes / grafting / CRISPR:** host susceptibility varies in this fungus family, so some individuals may be more tolerant, but **no resistant *Ficus* rootstock exists** and editing is 10+ years out — relevant only because **banking the germplasm (§7) keeps future options open.**
- **Electrotherapy / thermotherapy on standing trees:** not applicable (thermotherapy works only on cuttings — hence its use in §7's clean-up step).

## 9. The overriding constraint — structural safety

A declining 20 m tree near people/structures is, in arboricultural and legal terms, a **"living hazard."** This can override everything above:
- Commission a **written ISA Tree Risk Assessment (TRAQ) per tree**, triaged by what's underneath each one.
- If **Ganoderma/Phellinus conks** are at the base, or tomography/resistance-drilling shows major basal/buttress wood loss, **no treatment is curative** — the question becomes keep-with-mitigation vs. remove (saturated soil + wind is exactly where these fail).
- Because the genetics are banked (§7), **removing a dangerous individual ≠ losing it** — its clone survives. This also answers the local controversy over premature removals: bank first, then decide per tree on evidence.

## 11. Honest bottom line

The dramatic interventions (injections, sprays, a magic pollard) are the **weakest** bets. The unglamorous one — **fixing the roots and the water stress** — is the only one with real mature-tree recovery evidence, and it's the one almost nobody does. Salazar's clock assumes the stress continues; the case for saving these trees rests on breaking that, after first **ruling beetles in or out** (cheap) and **getting a per-tree risk read** (non-negotiable). You likely won't keep all five exactly as they are, and the most-decayed may have to come down for safety — but **slowing or arresting decline on the better trees is realistic, and preserving every one genetically is near-certain.**
